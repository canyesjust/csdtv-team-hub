CSDtv Cable & Connector Visual Dictionary

Reference Guide for Studio Interns

How to use this dictionary

This dictionary covers every cable and power connector you're likely to find in the CSDtv studio. Each entry shows what the connector looks like, what it's used for, and a tip to help you tell it apart from similar-looking ones.

The drawings are schematic — they show the shape and key features (number of pins, distinctive notches, relative size) but they're not photographs. When you encounter a connector for the first time, compare it to the drawing here, then if Justin asks, take a photo of the real one. Over time the dictionary fills up with real photos of CSDtv's actual gear.

If a cable in your hand doesn't match anything in here, don't guess — set it aside and ask Justin or Ryan. New connectors get added to this dictionary as they're encountered.

AC Power Connectors

IEC C13

Also called: Kettle lead, computer power cable

Look for: Rectangular black plastic socket with three slots inside. The most familiar power cord plug — same one your desktop PC uses. The matching male side (C14) lives on devices.

Used for: PCs, monitors, racks, power conditioners, switchers, anything with a removable cord. Rated for up to 10A typically.

Tip: The cable end you hold is C13 (female). The end that plugs into the device is C14 (male). If you say "kettle lead" anywhere on Earth, this is what people mean.

IEC C5

Also called: "Mickey Mouse", cloverleaf, trefoil

Look for: Three round holes arranged in a cloverleaf pattern. The shape literally looks like Mickey Mouse with two ears and a chin.

Used for: Laptop power adapters, some smaller monitors, projectors. Rated for lower amperage than C13.

Tip: If someone says "Mickey Mouse cable" or "cloverleaf," they mean this. Not interchangeable with C13 even though both are IEC — the holes are completely different shapes.

NEMA 5-15

Also called: Standard US wall plug, Edison plug, three-prong

Look for: Two parallel flat blades on top, one round ground pin below. The wall plug that comes out of every American outlet.

Used for: The other end of most power cords. Plugs into wall outlets, power strips, surge protectors, UPS units.

Tip: There's a slightly bigger version called NEMA 5-20 for 20-amp circuits — looks similar but one blade is sideways (T-shaped). You probably won't see 5-20 unless a high-amperage piece of gear requires it.

DC Power Connectors

DC Barrel Jack

Also called: Coaxial power connector, barrel plug

Look for: A cylindrical metal tip with a hollow center, attached to a cable. Comes in many sizes — the two most common in our studio are 5.5x2.5mm and 5.5x2.1mm.

Used for: Most DC power bricks for monitors, lights, audio gear, recorders. The brick converts AC from the wall to DC, then sends it down a cable ending in this barrel.

Tip: Size matters and isn't obvious by eye. 5.5x2.5mm and 5.5x2.1mm look identical until you try to plug them in. The number before the x is the outer diameter, the number after is the inner pin hole. ALSO: polarity matters — center positive vs center negative. The wrong one will damage gear. Check the brick's spec plate.

USB-C PD

Also called: USB-C Power Delivery

Look for: A USB-C connector (see USB section) being used to deliver power instead of data. Same physical shape, different purpose.

Used for: Modern laptops, tablets, phones, some cameras and monitors. Replacing barrel jacks across the industry.

Tip: Not all USB-C cables can deliver high wattage. A cable rated for 100W power delivery is different from a charge-only USB-C cable. Check labels.

D-tap

Also called: P-tap, PowerTap

Look for: A small black rectangular connector with two visible gold contact pads. D-shape from the side.

Used for: Camera batteries (V-mount, Gold-mount). Lets you tap power off the battery for accessories — monitors, transmitters, lights.

Tip: Always plug in with the correct orientation — the connector is keyed but it's easy to force backwards in low light. If it doesn't slide in smoothly, stop and check.

USB Connectors

USB-A

Also called: USB Type A, standard USB

Look for: A flat rectangular plug, about an inch wide. You've plugged a thousand of these in. The plastic insert inside is half the height of the metal outer shell — that asymmetry is why USB-A only goes in one way (the right way is always the second way you try).

Used for: The host end for keyboards, mice, flash drives, older phone chargers, audio interfaces, capture cards.

Tip: A USB-A plug with a blue plastic insert is USB 3.0 or higher. White or black insert is usually USB 2.0. Not foolproof but a quick visual hint.

USB-B

Also called: USB Type B, printer USB

Look for: Roughly square with a beveled top edge. Larger than micro or mini USB.

Used for: Printers, older audio interfaces, MIDI keyboards, some external drives.

Tip: You probably won't see this except on legacy gear. Almost all new equipment has moved to USB-C.

USB Micro-B

Also called: Micro USB

Look for: A small, flat, trapezoidal plug — wider on the top edge than the bottom. About a third of an inch wide.

Used for: Older Android phones, accessories, battery banks, some camera triggers and wireless receivers.

Tip: Slowly being replaced by USB-C across all device categories. If a device has Micro-B, it's probably 5+ years old.

USB Micro-B SuperSpeed

Also called: Micro USB 3.0

Look for: Like a regular Micro-B with an extra section glued onto the side, making it about twice as wide. Usually a blue plastic insert.

Used for: External hard drives, some older portable SSDs.

Tip: The extra section is what makes it USB 3.0. The Micro-B half can plug into a regular Micro-B port (in 2.0 mode), but the full SuperSpeed needs the matching wider port on the drive.

USB-C

Also called: USB Type C

Look for: A small, slim oval connector — about a third of an inch wide. Symmetric, so it plugs in either way (no more flipping it three times to find the right orientation).

Used for: Modern everything. Phones, laptops, tablets, cameras, monitors, audio interfaces, headphones. Will eventually replace USB-A entirely.

Tip: USB-C is the connector shape. What it actually carries depends on the cable and devices: data, power, video (Thunderbolt or DisplayPort Alt Mode), or all of the above. A "USB-C cable" can be anywhere from charge-only to 40Gbps Thunderbolt.

Apple Lightning

Also called: iPhone connector

Look for: A small, flat, thin connector with 8 visible gold contacts on each side. Slightly thinner than USB-C, no inner pin or socket.

Used for: Older iPhones (through iPhone 14), older iPads, AirPods charging cases.

Tip: Apple is moving away from Lightning to USB-C. Newer iPhones (15 and later) use USB-C.

Video Connectors

HDMI Type A

Also called: Standard HDMI, full-size HDMI

Look for: A flat trapezoidal connector, about a half-inch wide. 19 small pins inside.

Used for: The dominant video connector in consumer and prosumer gear. Cameras, monitors, switchers, TVs, projectors, capture devices.

Tip: The HDMI spec generations matter: Standard, High Speed, Premium High Speed (4K60), Ultra High Speed (4K120/8K, 48Gbps). The connector shape is identical across all of them. The cable jacket should print which.

HDMI Type C

Also called: Mini HDMI

Look for: Same trapezoidal shape as full-size HDMI, but about half the width. Found on cameras, tablets, some capture devices.

Used for: Smaller devices that can't fit a full HDMI port. Most mirrorless cameras have one of these.

Tip: Almost always cabled as Mini-to-A (the device has mini, the other end goes to a standard HDMI monitor or capture card).

DisplayPort

Also called: DP

Look for: Looks similar to HDMI but with one corner chamfered (cut diagonally) instead of fully symmetric. 20 pins.

Used for: Desktop computer monitors, graphics cards, some workstation laptops.

Tip: Often confused with HDMI on first glance. Look for the chamfered corner — that's the giveaway. There's also a Mini DisplayPort version (older MacBooks).

BNC / SDI

Also called: BNC connector

Look for: A round metal connector with two small notches near the base. Locks by pushing on and twisting a quarter turn — the bayonet lock.

Used for: SDI (Serial Digital Interface) video in broadcast. Our board meeting and livestream rigs use SDI. Also legacy analog video on some gear.

Tip: Push and twist. Don't pull straight off — you'll either fail to disconnect or damage the connector. The twist-lock is the whole point. 3G, 6G, and 12G are different SDI signal speeds — same connector, different cable spec inside.

VGA

Also called: 15-pin D-sub, blue plug

Look for: A trapezoidal connector with 15 pins in three rows of 5, two thumbscrews on either side. Usually blue.

Used for: Legacy analog video. Older projectors, older monitors, some KVMs.

Tip: Carries analog video only. Quality degrades over long runs. If you see VGA in 2026, the device is probably old enough to drive — make sure there's no digital option (HDMI or DP) you should be using instead.

RCA Composite

Also called: Yellow RCA, composite video

Look for: A round plug with a single center pin. Usually yellow when it's composite video. Red and white versions exist for analog audio (see Audio section).

Used for: Legacy analog video. VHS players, older DVD players, some older switchers.

Tip: If you see a yellow RCA, it's video. If you see red and white together, it's stereo audio. Same connector, different purposes — the color is the only visual hint.

Component Video

Also called: YPbPr, three-RCA bundle

Look for: Three RCA connectors bundled together, colored red, green, and blue.

Used for: Legacy analog HD video. Old HD camcorders, older game consoles, some broadcast monitors.

Tip: Always travels as a set of three — separating the colors gives you junk video. Don't mix with red/white/yellow composite cables, even though they look similar.

Audio Connectors

XLR-3 (Male)

Also called: Standard mic cable, three-pin XLR

Look for: A round metal connector with three pins arranged in a triangle. Has a small release tab on one side.

Used for: The standard for professional balanced audio. Microphones, speakers, audio interface inputs, mixer inputs.

Tip: The male end (pins) plugs into the female end (holes) of the next cable or device. Press the release tab to unlock. XLR cables can be daisy-chained — the directionality matters but is usually obvious by context (mic to mixer = mic XLR-female goes into mixer XLR-female via a cable that has male on one end, female on the other).

XLR-3 (Female)

Also called: Three-pin XLR female

Look for: Same round metal as the male end, but with three holes instead of pins.

Used for: The other end of every XLR cable. Microphones output female-style, gear inputs accept female.

Tip: Wait, microphones have female ends? Yes — the mic body has a male XLR socket built in, and the cable's female end connects to it. The far end of the cable (male) plugs into the mixer's female input. Easier to remember by example than by rule.

XLR-5

Also called: DMX cable, five-pin XLR

Look for: Same round housing as XLR-3, but with five pins (male) or five holes (female) instead of three.

Used for: DMX (lighting control) and AES/EBU (digital audio).

Tip: XLR-5 is most commonly DMX. Sometimes it's digital audio. Either way, don't substitute for analog audio — wrong impedance, weird results. Count the pins before plugging in.

1/4 inch TRS

Also called: Quarter inch, jack plug, headphone jack (large)

Look for: A cylindrical metal jack with rings around it. TRS (Tip-Ring-Sleeve) has two black rings, splitting it into three sections.

Used for: Balanced patch cables, instrument amps, headphones (some pro models), audio interface outputs.

Tip: Two black rings = TRS (stereo or balanced mono). One black ring = TS (mono unbalanced — instrument cables). Count the rings.

3.5mm TRS

Also called: 1/8 inch, mini jack, headphone jack

Look for: Same shape as 1/4 inch TRS but much smaller — about a quarter the size. Common headphone jack on phones, laptops, consumer audio.

Used for: Consumer headphones, lavalier mic outputs, computer audio, line-level connections to portable recorders.

Tip: TRRS (three rings) is a 3.5mm variant that adds a microphone channel for headsets with built-in mics. Counts of rings: 1 (TS, mono mic), 2 (TRS, stereo or balanced), 3 (TRRS, stereo with mic).

RCA (Audio)

Also called: Phono plug, red/white plugs

Look for: Same plug as composite video RCA, but for audio. Red and white pair = stereo line-level analog audio.

Used for: Consumer audio gear, turntables, some podcast mixers, mixer aux outputs.

Tip: Red is right channel, white is left. SPDIF coaxial digital audio also uses RCA — usually orange. If you see a single orange RCA, treat it as digital and label it red.

Speakon

Also called: NL2, NL4, NL8

Look for: A round black connector with a twist-lock collar. NL4 (4-pole) is the most common — four visible contact pads inside.

Used for: Powered speaker cables, amp-to-speaker connections. Standard in pro audio and touring.

Tip: Push, then twist to lock. The lock keeps a heavy speaker cable from disconnecting when something bumps it. NL2 (2-pole) and NL4 (4-pole) are most common; NL8 (8-pole) is for biamp installs.

TOSLINK

Also called: Optical audio, fiber audio

Look for: A square plastic connector. If the cable is live, you can see red light coming out of the end.

Used for: Digital audio over fiber. AV receivers, soundbars, some pro DACs and ADCs.

Tip: Carries digital signal as light. Don't bend the cable sharply — same rules as fiber ethernet. Always replace the dust cap when not in use.

TA4F

Also called: Mini-XLR, Switchcraft TA4

Look for: A tiny round connector with 4 pins, about a third the size of a regular XLR.

Used for: Wireless mic body packs — connects the lav or headset mic to the transmitter. Sennheiser, Shure, Lectrosonics all use variants.

Tip: Brand variants are not always interchangeable. A Sennheiser TA4F will fit a Shure body pack physically but may not be wired correctly. Always check brand compatibility before substituting.

MIDI 5-pin DIN

Also called: MIDI cable

Look for: A round metal connector with 5 pins arranged in a semicircle (not a full circle — only the top half).

Used for: Connecting MIDI keyboards, synths, and drum machines.

Tip: Legacy connector — modern MIDI gear usually uses USB. Still common in studio setups with vintage synths.

Ethernet Connectors

RJ45

Also called: Ethernet plug, Cat cable plug

Look for: A rectangular clear plastic plug with a release clip on top and 8 visible gold contact wires at the front.

Used for: Wired networking — everything that runs ethernet. Switches, cameras, network audio, streaming encoders.

Tip: The category (Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a, etc.) is determined by the cable, not the connector. The plug shape is identical across categories. Check the jacket print for the category, or use the cable tester.

Fiber LC

Also called: LC duplex

Look for: Two small square push-pull connectors locked together side by side. Each has a tiny exposed ferrule (the glass tip).

Used for: High-bandwidth network runs, long-distance ethernet, broadcast video over fiber.

Tip: Always store with dust caps on the ferrules. Never bend the cable tighter than a 4-inch loop — bends break the glass core. Older fiber variants (SC, ST) exist but LC is dominant in modern gear.

Quick recognition rules of thumb

  • Round connector with 3 pins or holes? → XLR-3 audio.
  • Round connector with 4 or 5 pins? → XLR-5 (DMX/AES) or Speakon (audio power) or specialty.
  • Round connector with bayonet (twist-lock) notches? → BNC for SDI video.
  • Trapezoidal flat connector? → HDMI (19 pins) or DisplayPort (chamfered corner).
  • Trapezoidal with 15 pins and thumbscrews? → VGA (legacy analog video).
  • Cylindrical jack with rings? → Audio TRS/TS. Count the rings: 1 = TS mono, 2 = TRS stereo/balanced, 3 = TRRS.
  • Cylindrical jack about 3mm wide? → 3.5mm audio. About 6mm wide? → 1/4 inch audio.
  • Rectangular with 8 contact wires and a release clip? → RJ45 ethernet.
  • Three-prong wall plug? → NEMA 5-15 AC power.
  • Three slots in cloverleaf pattern? → IEC C5 ("Mickey Mouse") AC power.
  • Square plastic with red light visible? → TOSLINK optical audio.
  • Cylindrical metal tip on a power cable? → DC barrel jack — and size and polarity both matter.
